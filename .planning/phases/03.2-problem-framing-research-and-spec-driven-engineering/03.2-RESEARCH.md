## Summary

Phase `03.2` is a bounded writing phase, not an architecture rewrite and not a framework deep dive. The guide already has a stabilized spine through the line before `# VI. Spec-Driven Engineering`, and the current `# VI` text is only a placeholder bridge. Planning should therefore focus on replacing that placeholder with one compact but substantive section that explains how good local-agent work depends on the sequence from framing to research to workflow design to explicit specs.

The planning center of gravity is not "cover all four topics equally." The context is explicit that specs should dominate. Framing, research, and workflow design matter here because they determine whether the eventual execution contract is clear enough to control scope, evidence, delegation, and verification. The section should therefore read as one argument about how explicit specs turn agent capability into reliable engineering work.

The main implementation risk is editorial drift. This phase can easily turn into a tutorial, a checklist, or an early GSD manual. The safer pattern is: preserve the verified boundary before `# VI`, keep the new section mostly conceptual, use only a few concrete examples, and make the ending hand off directly into GSD as the concrete framework example that operationalizes the design layer.

**Primary recommendation:** Plan `03.2` as a single-section replacement with 3-5 short subsections, a specs-first weighting, one or two carefully chosen examples at most, and explicit validation that `# VI` remains general while `# VII` stays the framework-specific realization.

### Pattern 1: Treat `# VI` As A Single Argument With Internal Stages
**What:** The section should explain framing, research, workflow design, and specs as a sequence that produces controlled execution.
**When to use:** For the overall section outline and paragraph ordering.
**Example:**
```markdown
# VI. Spec-Driven Engineering

Good agent work starts before execution.

Problem framing decides what is actually being solved.
Research gathers the evidence that prevents false assumptions.
Workflow design decides how work will be decomposed and checked.
The spec turns all of that into an explicit execution contract.
```

### Pattern 2: Give Specs The Longest, Sharpest Treatment
**What:** The section should spend the most detail on what specs do: bound scope, expose assumptions, define outputs, and make verification concrete.
**When to use:** In subsection sizing and paragraph emphasis.
**Example:**
```markdown
A spec is not paperwork. It is the control surface that tells the agent what is in
scope, what evidence matters, what artifacts should exist, and what counts as done.

Without that contract, execution quality depends too heavily on inference.
```

### Pattern 3: Use Minimal Examples Only To Resolve Abstract Ambiguity
**What:** Examples should clarify difficult concepts, not carry the section.
**When to use:** Only where the conceptual point would otherwise stay vague.
**Example:**
```markdown
Compare "improve onboarding" with "reduce first-run setup confusion by rewriting the
CLI setup section, preserving the existing command set, and verifying the path with a
fresh reader walkthrough." The second statement gives the agent a bounded target.
```

### Pattern 4: End With A Layered Handoff Into GSD
**What:** The final paragraph of `# VI` should make GSD feel like one concrete framework that operationalizes the workflow/spec layer just described.
**When to use:** In the closing transition into `# VII`.
**Example:**
```markdown
Once those contracts become explicit artifacts and repeatable workflows, they can be
packaged into a framework. That is the role GSD plays in the final section.
```

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- **Checklist drift:** Do not turn the section into numbered operating instructions for writing specs.
- **Framework leakage:** Do not explain GSD commands, artifacts, or lifecycle details inside `# VI`.
- **Symmetry for its own sake:** Do not force equal depth across framing, research, workflow design, and specs when the context says specs should dominate.
- **Boundary breakage:** Do not edit content before `# VI` unless a later explicit phase changes the guardrail.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Turning `# VI` into a mini spec-writing manual
**What goes wrong:** The section expands into procedures, templates, or step-by-step operator advice.
**Why it happens:** Specs are concrete, so the writing naturally drifts toward instruction.
**How to avoid:** Keep the section conceptual and explanatory; move any urge toward procedure to deferred work.
**Warning signs:** Imperative checklist language, large numbered lists, or repeated "first do X, then do Y" prose.

### Pitfall 2: Letting GSD swallow the section early
**What goes wrong:** The new section starts naming GSD artifacts, commands, or workflow stages before `# VII`.
**Why it happens:** GSD is the nearest concrete example of explicit planning and specs in this repo.
**How to avoid:** Speak in general local-agent engineering terms in `# VI` and reserve framework specifics for the next section.
**Warning signs:** `gsd-` command names, planning-file taxonomy, or lifecycle detail appearing before `# VII`.

### Pitfall 3: Explaining the four topics as peers instead of a chain
**What goes wrong:** The section reads like a taxonomy instead of a causal argument.
**Why it happens:** Outline symmetry is easy to draft.
**How to avoid:** Make each stage feed the next, with specs clearly presented as the contract produced by the upstream work.
**Warning signs:** Each subsection could be reordered without changing meaning.

### Pitfall 4: Reopening the stabilized spine
**What goes wrong:** Implementation edits bleed into the verified pre-`# VI` material.
**Why it happens:** The writer wants smoother setup or stronger earlier foreshadowing.
**How to avoid:** Treat the `03.1` boundary verifier as mandatory during execution.
**Warning signs:** Diff hunks outside `# VI`, or the boundary script fails after edits.

### Pitfall 5: Using too many examples
**What goes wrong:** The section becomes longer, more tactical, and less aligned with the surrounding guide voice.
**Why it happens:** Examples feel like an easy way to add clarity.
**How to avoid:** Add an example only when a concept remains fuzzy without it, and cap the phase at one or two examples.
**Warning signs:** Every subsection introduces its own scenario or comparison.
