# Phase 03.1 Architecture Diagnosis

## Current Spine

The current guide already has the right raw materials, but they do not yet read as one controlled argument.

Current flow:

1. `Introduction` explains the guide's subject at a high level.
2. `I. Agentic AI (the behavior)` defines the behavior in generic terms.
3. `II. Agentic Engineering (the discipline)` names the engineering concerns.
4. `III. Definitions` supplies the vocabulary.
5. `IV. Agentic Frameworks` becomes the first section that really explains the terminal-native environment model in concrete terms.
6. `V. Claude Code` gives the most legible worked harness example.
7. `VI. GSD As A Workflow Framework` adds workflow structure, but has to carry too much of the engineering-workflow perspective by itself.

The result is a guide whose strongest explanatory sections arrive late. The early spine is conceptually adjacent to the later sections, but not yet clearly preparing the reader for them.

## Coherence Problems

The first coherence problem is that the introduction does not yet make the scope boundary explicit enough. It says the guide is about local, tool-using environments, but it does not clearly distinguish development-time terminal-native agent work from production-system agents embedded in shipped products. That missing boundary matters because the later sections assume a development-time workspace model.

The second coherence problem is that sections `I-III` and `IV-VI` do not yet form a clean ladder. `I` defines agentic behavior broadly, `II` lists engineering concerns, and `III` defines terms, but `IV` is where the guide finally explains the environment model that actually organizes the rest of the document. That makes the early sections feel like a generic preamble rather than a minimal prerequisite set for the argument that follows.

The third coherence problem is overlap around loops, control, and architecture. `II` discusses control of the loop and architecture. `III` defines the harness. `IV` then explains the real operating loop and environment-level design implications. `V` restates the execution model through Claude Code. `VI` introduces another workflow layer on top. None of those sections are individually wrong, but their boundaries are blurry enough that the reader can feel repetition before the section roles are fully clear.

The fourth coherence problem is top-level sequencing. The roadmap now requires the engineering-workflow perspective to become equally central, but the current guide still jumps from the concrete harness example straight to GSD. That leaves no top-level place for framing, research, workflow design, or spec-driven engineering before the final workflow-framework example.

The fifth coherence problem is authority and guardrails. The repository still contains the historical pre-`IV` protected-prefix verifier from Phase `1`, while Phase `03.1` now explicitly owns targeted intro and `I-III` rework. Unless this phase states that reconciliation clearly, execution will be caught between an obsolete freeze and a newly approved structural refactor.

## Missing Or Thin Explanations

The most important missing explanation is the development-time versus production-system distinction. Without it, the guide tells the reader what surfaces exist in a local-agent environment, but not clearly enough what kind of agent setting the document is excluding.

The introduction is also thin as a reading map. It lists what the guide covers, but it does not yet explain the intended progression strongly enough: minimal concepts first, then environment patterns, then a concrete harness, then workflow design, then the final framework example.

Section `I` is thinner than it needs to be for the final guide spine. It defines agentic behavior generically, but not yet in a way that clearly hands off to engineering in real environments. That is not a call for wholesale expansion; it is a sign that the section may need tightening or reframing so it earns its place in the spine.

Section `II` is informative but broad. It names important concerns, yet it reads more like a topic inventory than a bridge into the environment-first body. The issue is not lack of detail alone. The issue is that the section's role in the whole argument is underexplained.

Section `III` has strong raw material, especially around harnesses, tools, skills, and memory, but it risks carrying definitions that repeat later sections unless its role is kept strictly to vocabulary and prerequisites.

The guide is also missing a top-level placeholder that tells the reader why workflow design belongs between the harness example and GSD. Right now that conceptual step exists only implicitly.

## What Actually Blocks Coherence

The blockers are not "more content everywhere" or "rewrite the whole guide." The real blockers are:

- the missing introduction boundary between development-time work and production systems
- the weak handoff from `I-III` into the environment-first body
- the absence of a top-level workflow-design slot before GSD
- the blurry section-role boundaries around loop, control, harness, and workflow structure
- the unresolved guardrail conflict around pre-`IV` edits

If those remain unresolved, the guide will continue to feel like a collection of correct sections rather than one argument with deliberate escalation.

## Out Of Scope For Phase 03.1

Several tempting additions would broaden the guide without improving coherence enough to justify them here.

Out of scope now:

- drafting the substantive Phase `03.2` chapter on problem framing, research, workflow design, and specs
- expanding the introduction into a broad comparison of agent categories or deployment architectures
- rewriting all of `I-III` from scratch just because the current versions are not yet perfectly shaped
- adding new framework comparisons, new concrete examples, or new tooling surveys
- doing the final compactness pass that belongs to Phase `4`

Those additions would consume attention that this phase needs for architecture, sequencing, and section-role clarity.

## Priority Triage

### Critical now

- Add an explicit introduction-level distinction between development-time terminal-native agent work and production-system agents.
- Re-state the guide as a reading path, not just a topic list, so the reader knows why the sections appear in this order.
- Diagnose and tighten the role of sections `I-III` so they function as minimal conceptual prerequisites rather than a generic preface.
- Reserve a full top-level slot before GSD for `Problem Framing, Research, And Spec-Driven Engineering`.
- Reconcile the historical protected-prefix freeze with this phase's authority to make targeted pre-`IV` edits.

These are the minimum changes required because each one directly supports coherence.

### Should improve now

- Reduce visible overlap between `II`, `III`, `IV`, `V`, and `VI` by clarifying what each layer owns.
- Improve transitions so the reader path becomes environment patterns -> harness example -> workflow-design layer -> final workflow-framework example.
- Strengthen only the thin explanations in `I-III` that are needed to support the later body.

These changes matter, but they should stay subordinate to the critical structural fixes above.

### Defer

- Full substantive teaching of framing, research, workflow design, and specs in Phase `03.2`.
- Final global tightening, compression, and newcomer-outcome verification in Phase `4`.
- Any broader expansion of definitions, framework taxonomies, or comparative discussions not required for this spine repair.

Deferral here is a scope-control mechanism, not an avoidance of real problems.

## Phase Scope Derived From The Triage

Phase `03.1` should own a controlled architectural refactor: diagnose the current spine, lock section roles, revise the introduction, make only the pre-`IV` changes that the diagnosis justifies, and insert the reserved top-level `03.2` slot before the final GSD section.

It should not become a freeform writing phase. Every added explanation must answer one of the critical or should-improve-now findings above. If an addition does not support coherence, it does not belong in this phase.

## Phase Risks

The first risk is under-planning. If this phase only reorders headings or inserts a placeholder section without fixing the conceptual holes named above, the guide will still have weak scope boundaries, late-arriving explanations, and unclear section roles.

The second risk is uncontrolled expansion. Once the diagnosis exposes thin explanations, it becomes tempting to fill every gap immediately. That would broaden the guide faster than it improves the argument and would effectively pull Phase `03.2` and Phase `4` work into this pass.

The correct phase posture is therefore narrow but not shallow: fix what blocks coherence, strengthen only what supports coherence, and defer everything else explicitly.
